Cyber risk, as the financial regulator sees it

US SEC Cybersecurity Disclosures

Since December 2024, US-listed companies must tell the SEC — in machine-readable form — how cyber risk affects their business, how their board oversees it, and when a material incident occurs. This is a board's-eye view of what they are saying. It is about business and financial impact, not technical metrics.

Why this matters for UK boards

The UK has no equivalent mandatory cyber-disclosure regime. UK-listed companies are not required to tell investors, in a structured way, whether cyber risk has materially affected them or how the board oversees it. But the underlying business risk is broadly the same on both sides of the Atlantic — and disclosure norms have a habit of crossing it. The US picture is therefore a useful preview: of the questions a board may soon be expected to answer, and of how comparable companies are already answering them.

Method & caveats

  • This measures disclosure, not security. A company saying cyber has not materially affected it is a statement to the regulator, not an audited fact about its defences.
  • Population. Rates are computed over annual reports (Forms 10-K and 20-F, including amendments), deduplicated to the latest filing per company and fiscal year.
  • Denominator. The materiality rate is among filers that answered the flag (yes ÷ (yes + no)); "not disclosed" filers are shown separately and excluded from the rate.
  • Recent cohorts are incomplete — the newest and oldest fiscal years contain only the filers that happen to fall in the data window, so read those bars with caution.
  • Source. SEC EDGAR Financial Statement and Notes Data Sets (the cyd: Inline-XBRL taxonomy). Self-reported and unaudited. Incident text is truncated by the SEC; follow the link for the full filing.
