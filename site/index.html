Cyber risk, as the financial regulator sees it

US SEC Cybersecurity Disclosures

Since December 2024, US-listed companies must tell the SEC — in machine-readable form — how cyber risk affects their business, how their board oversees it, and when a material incident occurs. This is a board's-eye view of what they are saying. It is about business and financial impact, not technical metrics.

Has it changed over time?

Share of annual filers reporting a material (or reasonably likely material) cyber impact, grouped by the calendar year in which each company's fiscal year ended.

US domestic vs. foreign issuers

Foreign private issuers file on Form 20-F; US domestic companies file on Form 10-K.

How boards govern cyber risk

Beyond impact, every filer must describe how cyber risk is governed. Among the … companies that disclosed their arrangements, the share affirming each practice in their latest annual report — a benchmark a board can hold itself against.

Where a bar is high, a board that cannot say "yes" is an outlier. Reporting cyber risk up to the board is the least universal of the five.

How complete is the governance picture?

How many of the five practices each company affirms. A measure of disclosure completeness — not a guarantee of the underlying security.

Reflects incidents tagged on US-domestic 8-K (Item 1.05) reports and any tagged foreign-issuer 6-K. Foreign private issuers report incidents on Form 6-K, which is rarely structured-tagged, so foreign-issuer incidents are under-represented here.

Why this matters for UK boards

The UK has no equivalent mandatory cyber-disclosure regime. UK-listed companies are not required to tell investors, in a structured way, whether cyber risk has materially affected them or how the board oversees it. But the underlying business risk is broadly the same on both sides of the Atlantic — and disclosure norms have a habit of crossing it. The US picture is therefore a useful preview: of the questions a board may soon be expected to answer, and of how comparable companies are already answering them.

Method & caveats

  • This measures disclosure, not security. A company saying cyber has not materially affected it is a statement to the regulator, not an audited fact about its defences.
  • Population. Current-state figures (the headline rate, the breakdowns and the governance scorecard) count each company once, using its most recent annual report (Forms 10-K / 20-F, incl. amendments). The over-time trend uses every year, one report per company per fiscal year.
  • Denominator. The materiality rate is among filers that answered the flag (yes ÷ (yes + no)); "not disclosed" filers are shown separately and excluded from the rate.
  • Recent year-ends are incomplete — filings arrive months after a company's year-end, so the most recent year-end bucket is still filling in and its rate is provisional.
  • Source. SEC EDGAR Financial Statement and Notes Data Sets (the cyd: Inline-XBRL taxonomy). Self-reported and unaudited. Incident text is truncated by the SEC; follow the link for the full filing.
